
## Session 09 — Adopt-a-Rothe

Day ???:

The exhaustion is real now. Not the kind you complain about — the kind that sits in your bones and makes your hands slow. The whole group is dragging. We haven't had a proper rest since the safe house, and that feels like a lifetime ago even though it's been less than a day.

Glabbagool came back. Oozed right up through the stone floor like nothing happened. Cheerful as ever, which is an unsettling quality in a block of sentient acid. The poor thing was too weak to keep up with us, so Amanita offered up a waterskin. We watched an entire gelatinous cube compress itself through the opening like an octopus squeezing through a crack. Now Amanita is carrying around a waterskin with an eyeball sloshing around inside it. Every party needs a little guy, I said. I stand by it.

I positioned myself centrally while the others scouted. Closed my eyes. Listened. The tunnels were quiet — just fungus and the occasional pop of gas pockets. No footsteps. No pursuit. For the moment.

Amanita found a pond. Of course she did. She's drawn to water and fungus the way I'm drawn to trouble. In the shallows were the bones of something large — a rothe, one of those massive beasts the drow use for hauling. Fused to its skeleton was a wooden chest, sealed with old magic. And behind it, this flickering doorway kept appearing and vanishing against the rock wall. A portal. Unstable. Sporadic.

The chest wouldn't open by force alone. Aeolus determined it needed a magic word. Amanita, being Amanita, channeled some wild energy through that mithril pickaxe of hers and pried the thing open anyway. Inside: a diamond and a spell scroll. Resurrection.

I said what I meant. If we had a real choice, I'd pick Topsy. We left her body sealed in that safe house — the stones rebuilt themselves around her like a tomb. Turvy hasn't been the same since. But the body was unreachable, and the rothe was right here, and Binks thought he could do it.

He did it. Called on his god, read the scroll with Blarg's help and his own guidance magic, and somehow channeled a spell three levels beyond anything he should be capable of. The bones grew muscle, grew fur, grew life. The whole beast just rose up out of the earth, chest still fused to its back, and promptly shoved Darby into a wall. Then it walked into the pond and started drinking like it had been dead for centuries. Which it probably had.

Her name is Roxanne. Eldeth read it off a dwarven collar tag. She doesn't remember anything — not her name, not this place, not dying. But she ate Amanita's rations and seemed content enough. Between the myconid sprout, the grieving wererat, the gelatinous cube in a bottle, and now a resurrected cow with a magic chest growing out of her spine, I am running a subterranean petting zoo.

Blarg checked the portal. Neutral origin, connected to the fungal network, destabilized by the corrupted faerzress. Could be stabilized temporarily. Then something rumbled from the other side — deep, earthy, throaty. Something heard us. Or something has been waiting.

We haven't decided what to do yet. But I know the look on Binks's face. He wants through that portal like a moth wants a flame.
